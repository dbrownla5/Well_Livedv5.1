## ORIGIN — THE SEATTLE STORAGE UNIT
*The real moment the business started. Not a metaphor. An actual thing that happened.*

Seattle. Dayna was dealing with her own flood. Kept bumping into people at the storage units — women in their 60s, women in their 40s, all of them exhausted. None of them were there because something good had happened. Someone had dumped stuff on them, or someone had passed, and now they were the one sorting it. A spouse's parent. A mother. A father-in-law. Always someone else's belongings becoming their problem.

Nobody looked grateful. Nobody felt lucky to have inherited the China, the furniture, the everything. It wasn't a gift. It was a weight.

And they all arrived at the same question: **what do we do with it?**

They have multiple generations of their own stuff already — wedding gifts, accumulated decades. Their kids don't want it. The kids are millennials and Gen Z, the kids don't have the space, the kids don't have the homes, the kids are shopping garage sales and refurbishing junk they think is a treasure while actual quality furniture sits unwanted. Even if the kids *did* want it, where would it go? Store it for twenty more years and then what? They still won't want it.

So what's the option? Hire a hauler. Pay someone to move it to a consignment store. Hope it sells. Take a loss. Donate the rest. Throw out the China because it's not a complete set — chipped a glass at a dinner in 1987, broke two plates at Thanksgiving, an estate planner won't touch it, an estate sale lumps it in with everything else.

**Dayna saw the thing nobody else was seeing:** there are people collecting that China piece by piece. Somebody, somewhere, is missing exactly the plate that got broken. The set isn't worthless — it's just routed wrong. The fur coat at Goodwill is worth ten times more if it comes with Gayle's story about wearing it to lunch at the Beverly Tower. The story is part of the value. The platform routing is part of the value. The willingness to sell one piece at a time on eBay or Facebook or 1stDibs — that's part of the value. Most people won't do it because it's too slow, too piece-by-piece, too much work for a $40 plate. Dayna will, because the $40 plate matters to someone, and the whole collection across a lifetime adds up. Somewhere, a collector is assembling that exact pattern from 1780s France, and one day the complete set lands at Sotheby's for a million dollars. The piece-by-piece is the strategy.

That's the founding observation. The business isn't organizing closets. It's recognizing value other people miss, routing belongings to people who actually want them, and carrying the story across the transfer so meaning isn't lost.

And it nests with everything else: she can do all of that *and* change a lamp, *and* patch a wall, *and* set up an Alexa, *and* answer your FaceTime in between, *and* run a Goodwill pickup, *and* flip half of what she picked up.

**The thesis line, in her own words:**
> "It's about transferring that and giving it all meaning and value — and I can do all of it and change your lamp and patch your wall and set up Alexa while I'm there, then answer your FaceTimes in between, and do Goodwill pickups, and flip half of it."

## 01 — THE CATEGORY PROBLEM
*Why this is hard to market.*

There is no existing category for what Dayna does. The client currently solves the problem by stringing together: TaskRabbit + a handyman + the Genius Bar + Poshmark + a moving company + a friend with a truck + a closet organizer + the donation pile no one drives away. That string is the problem. WLC replaces the string.

**Rule:** Never name the category in the headline. Name the problem so specifically the reader concludes the category exists by inference. The hero already does this: "One person for the move, the resale, the storage unit, and the other thing currently ruining your week."

**Cannot say:**
- "I do contracting" (no license, California $1,000 limit)
- "I do caregiving" (regulated)
- "I'm a handyman" (not — and flattens the brand)
- "I'm an organizer" (not — and flattens the brand)
- "Estate planning" / "legacy services" in the legal sense (not licensed)

**Must convey without saying:**
- Trusted in-between
- One person for the messy mix
- Already configured infrastructure (shipping, resale platforms, tools, storage)

## 14 — THE INHERITED STUFF PROBLEM
*The philosophical engine behind Legacy + Resale. Why this isn't estate planning.*

The cultural moment WLC is operating inside of:

The parent generation accumulated quality belongings on the assumption their children would want them. The children don't — not because the things aren't beautiful, but because the homes are smaller, the lives are more mobile, the aesthetic has shifted, and there's no space for a China hutch from 1962. The grandkids are shopping garage sales for things they think are treasures while the actual treasures sit unwanted in a storage unit.

What estate planners and estate sales miss:
- A chipped set is still valuable to someone collecting that pattern
- A fur coat with a worn-once story is worth more than a fur coat from Goodwill
- The piece-by-piece resale strategy is too slow for an estate sale but exactly right for the right buyer
- The story attached to an object is part of its value, not separate from it
- "Quality" doesn't disappear because the heir doesn't want it — it just needs to be routed to someone who does

WLC is the routing layer. The willingness-to-go-slow layer. The story-preservation layer. The "I'll sell it one plate at a time on eBay because someone is collecting that exact pattern" layer.

**This is why Legacy Inventory exists.** Not as legal estate work. Not as financial planning. As the thoughtful, piece-by-piece work of recognizing what's actually valuable, what story matters, what should be routed where, and what should be let go.

Provenance matters. The fur coat at Goodwill is worth one price. The fur coat with the story of where it was worn, who wore it, and what it meant is worth ten times that — to the right buyer.

**The practical claim:** when a client lets WLC sell their belongings, the buyer gets the story too. That's why pieces move faster, sell for more, and reach better homes. It's not a sentimental flourish — it's a pricing strategy backed by a value the seller couldn't access alone.

What makes it different from generic "competence": the human layer. No matter how long someone needs to talk through what they're feeling about the thing — she makes the time for that. Efficiency does not mean rushing the person. It means handling the operational layer so completely that the person has space to actually feel what they need to feel.

**The realization:**
This isn't a new business. It's the first time the thing she's been doing her whole life finally has a name, an invoice structure, and a market for it. Los Angeles, back in California, the right moment. The business fits because she's been training for it her entire life without calling it that.

**The thesis line, in her own words:**

**Why this matters for the brand:**
This is the answer to "why you and not someone else." Other organizers learned organizing. Other resellers learned reselling. Other handymen learned handyman work. Dayna has been doing all of this for everyone in her life, unpaid, for decades. The business is the moment she stopped doing it for free.

## 24 — FAST BAG FILL — OPERATIONAL FLOW
*Locked 5/27/26. The actual end-to-end process. Do not let copy drift back to vague language.*

**Who this is for:**
Busy people with platform-eligible inventory who'd sell it themselves if they had the time, the platforms, or the patience. Decent stack of clothes, designer pieces, accessories with real second-market value. Might already have a half-set-up Poshmark or Depop account. Done thinking about it.

**What this is NOT:**
- Not a donation pickup service
- Not a bulk textile recycling service
- Not a paid evaluation (free)
- Not paid at booking, ever
- Not a shortcut for unsalable items

**The end-to-end flow (8 steps):**

1. **Fill a bag** — Clothing, shoes, accessories, designer pieces. No prep, no sorting, no decisions.
2. **Schedule a pickup** — Free within LA service area. Use contact form to provide: number of bags, urgency, two preferred pickup time windows.
3. **Evaluation after pickup** — Dayna evaluates piece by piece on her time, not the client's.
4. **Client gets the list** — Three categories: items being listed, items routed to donation, items that are a judgment call.
5. **Optional decision window** — Client can weigh in on judgment-call items if they want. Or pass authority to Dayna entirely. This is offered at pickup as part of the eDoc release.
6. **eDoc release signed at pickup** — Items become WLC property until they sell OR until the 6-month check-in, whichever comes first.
7. **Listings go live** — Routed to the platform where the right buyer lives (Poshmark, eBay, Vestiaire, Chairish, Grailed, Etsy, Facebook Marketplace, Vinted, 1stDibs, private collectors).
8. **Payout when items sell** — Monthly report by the 1st, payout by the 5th via Venmo, Zelle, or check.

**Two signatures required:**
- **Resale Agreement** — signed after evaluation, before listings go live. Sets commission rate, payout terms, platform routing per category.
- **eDoc Release** — signed at pickup. Transfers possession to WLC until sale or 6-month check-in.

**Commission splits (matches Pricing page + vault Section 12):**
- Clothing & Accessories: 45% client / 55% WLC
- Designer & Luxury: 50% client / 50% WLC
- Pickup fee: $0
- Booking fee: $0

**6-month check-in:**
Only for items that haven't sold by then. Three options: relist, donate, return to client.

**Speed:**
Most pickups happen within 2–5 days of inquiry. Urgent pickups (moves, closeouts, time-sensitive transitions) usually doable inside 48 hours.

**What's NOT included in Fast Bag Fill:**
