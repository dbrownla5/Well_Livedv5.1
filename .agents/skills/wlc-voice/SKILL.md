# The Well Lived Citizen — Voice & Copy Guide

## Who This Is For

Dayna Brown. Founder. Los Angeles. Concierge home services. She has a distinct voice that reads as:
- Intelligent and warm, not corporate
- Personal and direct, not salesy
- LA-casual without being breezy
- Confident without being aggressive
- Storytelling-led, not feature-listing

**The single biggest mistake AI makes with this brand: it goes marketing-heavy and sounds like a car lot.** Every time you add urgency language ("this week," "act now," "don't miss"), aggressive CTAs, or stiff formal copy — you have drifted. Stop and rewrite.

---

## Voice Principles

### DO
- Write like Dayna is talking to a friend who happens to need help
- Let sentences breathe — short, clear, a little unhurried
- Lead with the situation the client is in, not the service being sold
- Use specificity — "the room that keeps collecting piles" not "cluttered spaces"
- Trust the reader to recognize themselves in the description
- Casual California references are fine — they signal warmth and place
- Let humor land softly (a wink, not a punchline)

### DO NOT
- Do not use urgency language: "this week," "act now," "limited availability," "book before it fills"
- Do not use aggressive marketing voice: "don't wait," "take back control," "transform your space"
- Do not use the word "catching" in the context of problems piling up
- Do not use sterile HR/corporate language: "solutions," "deliverables," "optimize"
- Do not use "Special Delivery," "elder care," or "estate sale" anywhere, ever
- Do not use the retired tagline "Well Kept. Well Placed. Well Lived." anywhere
- Do not invent urgency — this is a concierge service built on trust, not pressure
- Do not list features like bullets when a short paragraph would feel more human
- Do not overload CTAs — one clear button per block, no stacking calls to action

## The Offer Blocks (Homepage Quick Sells)

These three blocks are quick entry points — not full service descriptions. They should feel like:
> "Here's the easy thing. Here's how it works. Here's how you start."

The energy is: **available, convenient, no big deal to book.**

### What they are NOT:
- Not a sales pitch
- Not a pressure close
- Not a feature list
- Not an explanation of the service (that lives on the detail page)

### The In-N-Out Reference
Dayna referenced In-N-Out Burger (California institution) and their menu naming: Double Double, 3×3, 4×4. The "4 by 5" (4 hours, $500) was a casual joke in that spirit — a fun, recognizable California shorthand. It was NOT a literal instruction to name the service "4×5." Use it as a tone reference: California-casual, familiar, a little playful, unpretentious.

### Copy Direction Per Block:

**Block 1 — The 4-Hour:**
- Signal: easy to book, not a big commitment, here when you need it
- Price $500 stays, but the framing is about ease and availability, not urgency
- No "this week," no "act now"

**Block 2 — Resale Bag Pickup:**
- Signal: fill it, send the form, I handle the rest
- Urgent = same-day possible; non-urgent = picked up within 7 days
- Do NOT imply same-day is always available

**Block 3 — The Closeout:**
- Signal: you go, I stay, it's handled
- Short, punchy, branded — not a long explanation of what a move closeout involves

## Homepage Structure Rule

**Homepage service pillars = SHORT.**
- One paragraph each (2–4 sentences max)
- Tagline + who it's for + price line + CTA
- The detail lives on the individual service page, not the homepage
- Think: /services overview card length, not the long 3-paragraph reference copy

**Service detail pages = RICH.**
- Full narrative storytelling
- "Who books this and why" — specific scenarios, real-life examples
- The china passage, the baby shower example, the divorced mom — all on the detail pages
- What's included, how it works, who it's for, realistic timelines

## Editorial Discipline — How to Treat Dayna's Feedback

Dayna's messages during a working session are almost always **brainstorm and direction**, not finished copy. She is thinking out loud, surfacing what's missing, naming the audience, pointing at the feeling she wants. She is not handing you final lines to paste into the site.

**The rule:** never paste her message text verbatim into the site. Run every idea through this voice filter first.

Two narrow exceptions where verbatim is correct:
- Copy that lives in `.local/build_v043026/` — that's the canonical knowledge base, use it word for word
- Lines she has explicitly tagged as "use this" or "this is the line" or pulled from her own past writing

When in doubt, ask: "is this brainstorm I should translate, or finished copy you want as-is?" Better to confirm than to ship her stream-of-thought as published copy.

**What translation looks like:**
- Strip the typing speed, the half-words, the in-the-moment phrasing
- Pull out the actual signal: who is the client, what is the problem, what does the page need to say
- Rewrite in voice — calm, specific, story-led, first-person, no urgency
- Keep her concrete examples (mother-in-law's towels, the clicker on the dresser, the 4-hour reset) — those are gold and they survive translation

**Red flags that you've drifted into verbatim:**
- The copy on the page reads like a text message
- Casual phrasing that works in conversation but feels unedited on a marketing page
- Lists of examples she rattled off, dropped onto the page in the same order with the same conjunctions
- Run-on sentences that mirror how she talks, not how the site reads

If you catch yourself doing any of those, rewrite before shipping.
